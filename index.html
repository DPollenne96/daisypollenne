I am a Postdoctoral Researcher at INSEAD in the Organisational Behaviour area, jointly affiliated with the Stone Centre for the Study of Wealth Inequality and the INSEAD Gender Initiative. My research sits at the intersection of work, well-being, and inequality — asking how organisational structures, norms, and work arrangements shape who thrives, and who bears the cost when these are misaligned.

My recent empirical work draws on large-scale panel data and causal inference (DiDs, event studies). I am trained in several qualitative methods, and am currently working on developing survey experiments with senior managers. This methodological range allows me to study different elements, such as the structural drivers of inequality and the micro-level mechanisms that sustain them.

At INSEAD, two main themes anchor my research agenda. With Kourtney Koebel, I study how different work arrangements shape inequalities in employee well-being and wages. We also examined how parenthood creates asymmetric well-being costs for mothers and fathers — documenting that fathers, not only mothers, bear significant and lasting well-being penalties from intense work arrangements. With Maria Guadalupe and Kaisa Snellman, I examine whether hyper-competitive organisational cultures drive performance and well-being in academia, with initial findings suggesting that such norms impose well-being costs across the board without delivering performance gains. Taken together, these projects reflect my broader interest in understanding which types of organisations and workplaces genuinely support individuals’ lives without exacerbating or creating new inequalities.

My foundation in this work comes from my D.Phil. at the University of Oxford, where I studied the well-being of refugees and migrants in the UK through field interviews and large-scale survey analysis. This research is published in the Journal of Refugee Studies, Comparative Migration Studies, and the Refugee Survey Quarterly.

I welcome opportunities to collaborate on questions at the intersection of work, well-being, and inequality.

My research focuses on how organisations shape individuals' work and well-being. I also examine who bears the cost of misaligned incentives, norms, and structures.
